🏉 Cillian's Speed Plan

A simple speed plan you can do yourself, twice a week. Tap the tabs, then tick off each drill as you go.

How to use this

Two sessions a week is plenty. Leave at least one day between them so your body recovers. Every session has the same shape:

  • Warm-up — always the same World Rugby Activate warm-up, about 15 minutes.
  • The main speed work — this is where you actually get faster.
  • A quick finish and cool-down.
The golden rule of speed: when you sprint, sprint properly, then rest fully before the next one. Speed is built when you are fresh, not when you are tired. If you find yourself slowing down, the session is done. Quality beats quantity every single time.
📍 You only need a flat patch of grass and a handful of cones or markers — jumpers, water bottles, anything works.

Your warm-up

Do this before every session — tick each part off.

Use the World Rugby Activate U15 warm-up from the booklet your uncle gave you. It is built exactly for your age and it gets your body ready to move fast and safely. Work through the four parts in order:

0%

Activate has four versions (Phase 1 to Phase 4) that get gradually harder. Start on Phase 1 and 2 for the first few weeks, then move on to Phase 3 and 4 once they feel easy. The booklet shows every drill with a photo.

Note: the booklet introduces some harder exercises (like the Nordic Hamstring Curl) from Phase 2 onwards. Do those with a partner, and not the day before or the day of a match. Your hamstrings need to be fresh to play.

Session 1: Acceleration

Getting quick off the mark.

This is the most useful kind of speed in rugby — exploding from a standstill to beat someone or chase a ball. Do this after your Activate warm-up.

Golden rule: sprint fresh. When the sprints slow down, you are done.

Main work

Rest until you feel fully recovered between every sprint. Walk back slowly, take 60 to 90 seconds. There is no rush.

Cone layouts: all of these are straight-line runs. Put one cone at the start and one at the finish, set at the distance shown (15 m, 20 m). For March then run, you can add a middle cone at 10 m to mark where you switch from marching to running.

Session 2: Top speed & footwork

Running fast and changing direction.

This session has two halves: pure running speed, then the quick feet you need to step and beat a defender. Do this after your Activate warm-up.

Golden rule: be fresh for the speed work. Quality beats quantity.
0%

Part 1: Running speed

Full recovery between every run: walk back, take 90 seconds or more. This is the part of the session where you must be fresh.

Cone layouts: straight-line runs again. Build-up runs use a start and finish cone 40 m apart. Flying sprints use three cones: a start cone, one at 10 m (where you hit full speed), and one at 30 m (where you ease down).

Part 2: Footwork and stepping

Cone layouts: Ladder feet is 6 cones in a straight line, roughly one foot apart, run with quick small steps. Z-run is 4 cones in a zig-zag pattern about 5 m apart, sprinting and cutting between them. Mirror steps needs no cones, just space to sprint and side-step.

Finish

Tips to actually get faster

  • Sprint fresh, not tired. The first sprints of the session are your best. Once they get slower, stop. You have done the job.
  • Rest more than feels necessary. Top sprinters rest a long time between efforts. Resting is part of the training, not a break from it.
  • Run tall and relaxed. Tense shoulders and a clenched jaw slow you down. Stay loose.
  • Pump the arms. Fast arms drive fast legs. Drive the elbows back hard.
  • Sleep and food matter. You get faster while you rest, especially with good sleep. This is where the gains happen.
  • Be patient. Real speed gains take weeks and months, not days. Keep showing up.

A note on staying safe

A few simple rules:

  • Never skip the warm-up. Sprinting cold is the fastest way to pull a muscle.
  • If something hurts (not the normal burn of effort, but a real pain, especially in a hamstring or groin), stop and tell an adult. Do not run through it.
  • If you played a hard match or trained heavily, it is fine to skip a session or go easy. Listen to your body.

If your legs feel heavy or you are still sore from the last session, do the warm-up only and leave the sprints for another day.

Where this comes from

The warm-up is World Rugby's Activate U15 Injury Prevention Exercise Programme, a genuine, age-specific World Rugby resource. It is the warm-up foundation for every session here.

The sprint and footwork sessions are built on well-established principles of youth athletic development and sprint training: technique and short distances before volume, full recovery between maximal efforts, low total sprint volume for a developing athlete, and acceleration work prioritised because it transfers most directly to rugby. These sessions are principle-based and age-appropriate. They are not copied from, or formally badged by, a specific IRFU or World Rugby published document. For officially branded sprint material, source it directly from their current resources and verify before relying on it.
